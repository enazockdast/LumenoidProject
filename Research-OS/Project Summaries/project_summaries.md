# Research Project Summaries

**Overarching Goal:** Relate lumenoid growth, cell movement, and multicellular organization to the active and passive forces — adhesion, actomyosin contractility, cell growth, and proliferation — that generate them. Across all projects, tissue shape, cell dynamics, and sorting are ultimately expressed through a unified *cell state representation* linking molecular perturbations to tissue-scale observables.

**Explanatory Modeling Framework.** The program operates in two phases that feed into each other. In the *hypothesis-driven phase* (now), fast cycles of experiment → segmentation/tracking → hypothesis → forward model drive identification of the dominant physical parameters for each process. Models are kept minimal and interpretable; perturbations are the primary tool for disambiguation. In the *scientific ML phase*, the large datasets enabled by the label-free nuclear segmentation and cell tracking pipeline (under development) enable a shift toward data-driven inference. This phase splits into two stages: **Phase 2a** (parameter inference, near-term) uses known model forms — active polar fluid, vertex model, Langevin shape equation — and infers constitutive parameters directly from data; **Phase 2b** (equation discovery, longer-term, contingent on data scale-up) uses sparse regression or PINNs to identify which terms and fields actually govern the dynamics without assuming the model form. Running either stage across the perturbation series traces trajectories in parameter space, progressively triangulating the cell "energy" or state landscape — the effective free energy whose minimization (or steady state) defines tissue organization.

## LumenoidGrowth

*Status: manuscript in preparation.*

### Measure
Time-lapse imaging tracks inner radius, outer radius, and epithelial thickness of hiPSC lumenoids over days. The central observation — that the epithelium *thickens* while the lumen grows, the opposite of pressure-driven thinning seen in most organoids — is quantified across control, rapamycin (reduced growth), and TJP1/ZO-1 CRISPRi knockdown (increased permeability) conditions. Rapamycin reduces thickening; ZO-1 knockdown shrinks the lumen and enhances thickening.

The next experimental layer targets mechanisms and competing timescales directly. **Mosaic TJP1/control lumenoids** test how local permeability heterogeneity propagates into global shell mechanics and lumen pressure. **DOX washout experiments** measure the system's response time to step changes in cell state, resolving the competition between osmotic relaxation, elastic remodeling, and growth timescales. **Actomyosin perturbations** — pharmacological (blebbistatin, Y-27632) and iCRISPRi (ROCK1, MYH9, MYH10) — test whether active cortical tension contributes to shell mechanics independently of passive elasticity. **Actin and E-cadherin / N-cadherin intensity fields** measured alongside morphometrics connect cytoskeletal organization and adhesion to the mechanical state of the shell, and serve as molecular inputs for the SciML pipeline.

Growth rate is the central control parameter and can be modulated through three independent routes: **rapamycin** (mTOR inhibition), **glycolysis inhibitors** (2-DG or similar, distinct metabolic node), and **Dhiraj's synthetic growth control circuits** (synthetic biology constructs that directly set cell-cycle entry or exit rate). Using all three in parallel is essential: if thickening is truly growth-rate-driven, all three methods should produce quantitatively consistent thickness reductions when matched by rate — any discrepancy reveals a contribution from the specific perturbed pathway beyond its effect on growth alone.

### Model
The manuscript is built on the **minimal continuum model**, which couples: (1) cell volume growth, (2) osmotic transport (active pumping, passive leakage, water flow), and (3) elastic shell mechanics (epithelial stiffness + basement membrane). The key insight: when cell volume is added faster than osmotic and elastic relaxation accommodate, the shell is area-compressed; cell incompressibility converts area compression to thickness increase. The lumenoid operates in a *strong osmotic flow* regime.

Post-publication, the continuum model will be extended to **Voronoi/vertex models** that resolve individual cells, implementing the full adhesion matrix (J_AA, J_BB, J_AB), junctional edge tensions (actomyosin contractility Λ_ij), cell growth and division rules, and cell-state labels. This resolution enables perturbation experiments to be implemented mechanistically rather than through effective parameters, bridges to CellSorting and Cell Shape Dynamics, and is the natural target for Phase 2a SciML: given morphometric time series and actin/cadherin intensity fields, infer how junctional energy coefficients depend on measured molecular quantities.

### Predict
(1) Faster growth → thicker shell; slower growth → thinner shell (validated). (2) Increasing permeability strongly shrinks lumen radius and increases thickness — strong-flow regime (validated). (3) All three growth-rate perturbations should produce matched thickness reductions when calibrated by rate; isoform-specific effects will appear as deviations. (4) Mosaic TJP1 lumenoids: global dynamics respond to average permeability, but local mechanical heterogeneity produces measurable thickness gradients. (5) DOX washout reveals the timescale hierarchy: if osmotic relaxation is fast relative to elastic remodeling, lumen radius responds before thickness; if elastic is fast, the reverse. (6) Actomyosin perturbations change effective shell stiffness independently of growth rate, producing thickness-radius trajectories distinguishable from rapamycin effects.

### Build
The continuum model maps morphology across the growth rate × permeability parameter space, identifying regimes where thickening vs. lumen shrinkage dominates. The post-publication vertex extension will map how spatial heterogeneity in cell state (growth rate, stiffness, permeability) propagates to global shell shape and will support Phase 2a SciML inference of junctional energy coefficients from actin and cadherin intensity fields.

### Validate
Control dynamics reproduced quantitatively. Rapamycin and ZO-1 datasets validated. Open: (i) single-cell volume measurements (incompressibility test); (ii) parameter identifiability analysis; (iii) mosaic, washout, and actomyosin perturbation datasets; (iv) vertex model predictions against cell-resolved morphometric data (post-publication).

## Cell Shape Dynamics

*Status: active experiments and model development; incorporates the former CellDynamics and CellShape projects.*

### Measure

**Collective cell motion.** Two regimes of organized collective motion span lumenoid morphogenesis. In the pre-lumenoid **2D planar colony**, cells rotate coherently CCW (label-free brightfield nuclear tracking, ZSD-20×). On the **3D lumenoid surface** (lattice light-sheet), projected nuclear velocity fields organize into vortices and spirals with point-source or point-sink cores. Two 3D datasets exist with surface streamlines computed.

**Cell and nuclear shape.** Cell shape evolves from flat/cuboidal (2D colony) to columnar/pseudostratified (3D lumenoid). Observables: cell height, apical-basal aspect ratio, nuclear z-position, and their evolution with lumenoid size, growth rate, and perturbation. A dedicated **apical-basal nuclear motion** track uses existing LLS nuclear trajectories to resolve z-displacement as a function of cell-cycle phase — quantifying the INM program that the active polar fluid model identifies as the source of surface-divergence spirals.

**Molecular fields.** **Actin intensity** and **E-cadherin / N-cadherin intensity fields** are tracked alongside nuclear trajectories. Actin orientation and magnitude provide a spatial proxy for the polarity field **p**; cadherin distributions inform the adhesion landscape and serve as alternative force descriptors. Together these constitute the measured cell state inputs for the SciML inference pipeline. A key intermediate question — answerable before the full SciML pipeline is ready — is whether actin intensity correlates with local velocity magnitude and direction, and whether disrupting actin (actomyosin perturbations) or adhesion (cadherin knockdowns) changes that correlation. This determines which molecular field is the best descriptor of the active driving force.

**Perturbations** dissect the contributions of growth rate, active stress, cell mechanics, and adhesion: rapamycin, glycolysis inhibitors, Dhiraj's synthetic growth circuits (growth rate); ZO-1 knockdown and mosaic ZO-1 (mechanics + permeability); ROCK1, MYH9, MYH10 via iCRISPRi and pharmacological actomyosin perturbations; E-cadherin and N-cadherin induction/knockdown.

**2D pre-patterning geometries.** Confining colonies to printed stripes, concentric disks, and other shapes before the 3D transition imposes defined boundary conditions and symmetry constraints on polarity and velocity fields. Prior experiments show that **a stripe geometry seeds a hollow cylinder with two spherical caps** rather than a sphere, and **a concentric ring seeds a torus**. This gives a direct experimental handle on how 2D boundary geometry propagates into 3D morphological topology — a constraint no molecular perturbation can provide. In the active polar fluid model, different confinement geometries impose different boundary conditions and force different defect arrangements; in the Langevin shape framework, different geometries impose different effective boundary potentials on the shape modes.

**48-hour lattice light-sheet runs** (>1 cell division, planned) will resolve nuclear volume growth, cell count, streamline evolution with time/size R, and correlation of radial nuclear motion with mitosis — the direct INM source test.

### Model
Three complementary frameworks operate at different resolutions.

*Active polar fluid (continuum, current default for collective flow).* The cell layer is a **chiral active polar/nematic gel** on S². Active and chiral stresses driving CCW rotation in 2D drive vortices on the sphere (Poincaré–Hopf theorem forces +1 defects). The active driving force is a **traction/self-propulsion term** **f** = γv₀**p** (monopolar; motivated by traction-force microscopy in confluent epithelia). Pure in-plane incompressible flow gives only closed vortex loops; spirals require out-of-plane nuclear flux (INM) as a surface divergence source. Solved pseudospectrally (IMEX, toroidal + spheroidal decomposition on S²). The **flat-geometry limit** is used first for rapid exploration before committing to the full spherical solver.

*Voronoi/vertex model (cell-resolved, for perturbations and shape).* The vertex framework from LumenoidGrowth is extended with polarity dynamics and active traction, giving cell-resolved simulations directly comparable to tracked nuclear trajectories, actin fields, and cadherin distributions. Better suited for mosaic perturbation experiments, cell-cycle-gated events (mitosis, INM), and explicit cell shape outputs (height, aspect ratio, nuclear z-position).

**Shape Dynamics** *(subproject, Langevin description of cell and nuclear shape).* Each cell and nucleus is represented by a small set of shape parameters via *spherical harmonic decomposition* (3D) or PCA of shape mode space (2D and 3D). The leading coefficients q = (q₁, q₂, ..., q_n) capture the dominant degrees of freedom — volume, elongation, bending, higher-order deformations — with typically n ≲ 10 modes capturing >90% of variance. Their dynamics are modeled as an overdamped Langevin equation:

    dqᵢ/dt = −∂E(q, neighbor states)/∂qᵢ + ηᵢ(t)

where E is an effective energy depending on the cell's own shape modes and those of its neighbors, and η encodes active and thermal fluctuations. Fitting this to tracked shape trajectories infers E(q) and the noise statistics directly from data — the cell shape analog of the broader SciML energy landscape program. The neighbor-coupling term captures how mechanical interactions (adhesion, junction tension) constrain shape and how shape changes propagate through the tissue. Shape Dynamics shares the same Langevin framework as CellSorting's pattern inference, applied here to shape mode observables rather than sorting patterns.

*Phase 2a SciML (parameter inference, near-term).* Given measured velocity **v**(x,t), actin intensity I_a(x,t), and cadherin intensity I_c(x,t): learn the mapping I_a → **p** and simultaneously infer activity ζ, chirality κ, Frank constant K, and traction coefficient γv₀ via sparse regression (SINDy-style: assume model form, regress for coefficients as functions of measured fields) or PINNs (embed PDEs as constraints, learn **p**(x,t) and parameters jointly). For the vertex model: learn E(cell state) — how adhesion and line tension terms depend on measured molecular fields. Running inference across the perturbation series maps a trajectory in (ζ, κ, K, γv₀) space per condition.

*Phase 2b SciML (equation discovery, longer-term, contingent on label-free data scale-up).* Remove the assumption of a known model form; discover which terms and fields govern the dynamics from data. Identifies whether actin, cadherin, mitosis, or combinations thereof are the minimal sufficient descriptors of collective motion and shape.

### Predict
(1) Angular velocity ω(r) in the 2D colony rescales with colony size via the ratio of colony radius to active screening length; reversing chirality reverses rotation sense and 3D spiral handedness simultaneously. (2) Surface divergence ∇·v is non-zero and localized at spiral cores; blocking INM converts spirals to pure vortices. (3) Streamline topology changes with lumenoid size R in a near-parameter-free way. (4) Rapamycin and glycolysis inhibitors reduce both flow magnitude (less growth-driven active stress) and spiral intensity (fewer mitotic INM events); Dhiraj's synthetic circuits provide an independent test. (5) Actin intensity correlates with the polarity field **p** throughout the tissue — the key discriminating test between monopolar traction (correlation everywhere) and dipolar active stress (correlation only near defects). E-cadherin and N-cadherin fields will be tested as alternative or complementary force descriptors. (6) Stripe-patterned colonies seed hollow cylinders with two spherical caps; concentric-ring colonies seed toroidal lumenoids. Both are topologically equivalent to a sphere (χ = 2, 2 defects required); the capped cylinder differs from the disk-seeded sphere only in geometry, not topology. Collective flow on a capped cylinder vs. torus will show qualitatively different defect structures that the active polar fluid model should reproduce from geometry alone. (7) Spherical harmonic decomposition of nuclear shapes will show that low-order modes (elongation, bending) have longer relaxation times (shallower potential wells) than high-order modes, and that neighbor coupling is detectable as spatial correlations in shape fluctuations. (8) Phase 2a SciML inference will reveal whether (ζ, γv₀, κ) change independently across perturbation conditions or co-vary low-dimensionally, indicating the underlying cell state variable.

### Build
The pseudospectral S² solver maps the aster/vortex/spiral phase diagram across activity, chirality, and source strength. Phase 2a SciML extends this to a *data-driven phase diagram*: inferred parameters per condition are placed on the theoretical diagram, testing whether experiments visit the predicted regimes. The vertex model simulates mosaic protocols and cell-cycle-gated events before experiments are run. The Langevin Shape Dynamics framework will be built as a shared inference tool usable across Cell Shape Dynamics and CellSorting once the shape quantification pipeline exists. Simulating the Langevin model in stripe, disk, ring, and toroidal confinement geometries will map how geometry shifts accessible shape modes and timescales, prioritizing which pre-patterning experiments to run.

### Validate
Solver validated (9/9 tests); point-source Green's function to <10⁻³. Experimental sequence: (i) ω(r) profile across 2D colony sizes vs. active-nematic prediction; (ii) surface divergence at spiral cores; (iii) size-R scaling of streamlines across 48-hour runs; (iv) mitosis-streamline correlation; (v) rapamycin, ZO-1, and actomyosin perturbation responses against a single parameter set; (vi) actin and cadherin intensity correlations with velocity fields; (vii) stripe and ring geometry outcomes vs. topological predictions; (viii) Phase 2a inferred parameters placed on theoretical phase diagram; (ix) Langevin E(q) validated by predicting shape relaxation times and neighbor correlations in held-out data.

## CellSorting

*Status: active experiments and model development.*

### Measure
Two hiPSC populations are mixed in a lumenoid; one carries a DOX-inducible N-cadherin construct. The measurement battery tracks: membrane-localized N-cadherin intensity, heterotypic contact fraction, domain interface length and size distribution, neighbor-exchange rate, cell mean-squared displacement, and pattern autocorrelation — all as a function of time and DOX history. **E-cadherin intensity** is tracked in parallel as a baseline endogenous adhesion reference and as a potential independent descriptor of pairwise interaction energies. **Actin intensity** is measured alongside cadherin fields to connect contractility perturbations to spatial cytoskeletal reorganization.

The immediate priority is **DOX washout kinetics**: vary DOX concentration, induction duration, and washout time to fit a minimal response model (dN/dt = k_on f(C_DOX) − k_off N) and determine delay, saturation, and decay timescales. This is prerequisite for any sorting interpretation — the competition between protein turnover and tissue rearrangement timescales must be resolved first. The second layer tests **pattern reversibility**: induce until partial sorting, mature sorting, or full domain formation, then remove DOX and track whether the pattern persists, dissolves, or roughens. The third layer applies **actomyosin perturbations** (ROCK1, MYH9, MYH10 via iCRISPRi; blebbistatin, Y-27632 pharmacologically) to determine whether differential contractility independently generates or reshapes sorted domains.

All measurements — N-cadherin, E-cadherin, actin, nuclear trajectories, neighbor identities — form the **cell state representation**: a per-cell vector encoding adhesion, contractility, motility, and polarity as functions of position and time. This is the common input to the SciML inference framework shared across all projects.

### Model
Cell sorting is a dynamical self-organization process: cell state → adhesion/contractility/motility → rearrangement → new neighbors → updated signaling/state. The immediate model is an **agent-based / Voronoi-vertex simulation** with time-dependent adhesion (from measured induction/washout kinetics), independent edge tension for contractility (Λ_ij(t)), and proliferation. Parameter sweeps map regimes: rapid remixing, slow domain dissolution, interface roughening, kinetic trapping. A longer-term extension adds **neighbor-dependent signaling** (lateral inhibition, contact-dependent fate) to close the mechanics-signaling feedback loop.

The Phase 2a SciML extension infers E(cell state) — how adhesion and line tension terms in the vertex model depend on measured N-cadherin, E-cadherin, and actin fields — from time-lapse videos of mosaic lumenoids. Running this across the perturbation series maps how the energy landscape deforms: which perturbations shift the system between basins (pattern class changes) vs. change kinetics within a basin (timescale changes for the same pattern type). Phase 2b SciML will determine whether N-cadherin and E-cadherin are sufficient descriptors of J_ij or whether additional fields (actin, polarity) are needed.

### Predict
(1) Fluid lumenoids remix after DOX washout once N-cadherin levels converge; jammed lumenoids retain domains. Domain persistence correlates with neighbor-exchange rate, not instantaneous sorting index. (2) Fast induction relative to rearrangement creates a mechanically heterogeneous but spatially mixed tissue initially. (3) MYH9 and MYH10 knockdowns produce distinct interface phenotypes not capturable as effective adhesion changes. (4) Lateral inhibition coupled to heterotypic adhesion stabilizes salt-and-pepper organization; strong adhesion traps signaling defects. (5) Phase 2a SciML will reveal whether N-cadherin and actomyosin perturbations deform the energy landscape independently (orthogonal axes) or are correlated — a test of whether adhesion and contractility are separable descriptors of sorting.

### Build
The Voronoi/vertex model maps the (adhesion, contractility, motility, fluidity, proliferation) space onto pattern classes and timescales, prioritizing experiments that probe the most discriminating regions. Phase 2a SciML maps the inferred energy landscape across perturbation conditions toward a predictive map from (N-cadherin level, E-cadherin level, actin intensity, proliferation rate) to (pattern class, timescale, reversibility).

### Validate
DOX-inducible N-cadherin sorting demonstrated in 2D colonies and spheroids; agent-based models stable and fast enough for parameter sweeps. Pipeline: (i) fit DOX response model; (ii) pattern reversibility at partial vs. mature sorting; (iii) time-dependent adhesion model vs. washout data; (iv) ROCK1/MYH9/MYH10 feasibility; (v) matched adhesion vs. contractility experiments. Phase 2a validation: inferred J_ij(t) and Λ_ij(t) should recover independently measured cadherin and actin intensities; inferred energy landscape should predict pattern class and timescale for held-out perturbation conditions.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### Cell State Representation
All three projects measure overlapping quantities on the same cells: nuclear trajectories, actin intensity, E-cadherin and N-cadherin levels, cortical tension proxies, cell-cycle phase, and neighbor identities. A unified per-cell state vector encodes these as functions of position and time. A perturbation (DOX, rapamycin, actomyosin knockdown) maps onto a trajectory in this state space; tissue-scale outcomes (thickening, sorting pattern, collective flow topology, cell shape regime) are readouts of that trajectory. The cell state representation is both the input to the SciML inference pipeline and the common language that makes the three projects a single program.

### Modeling Resolution Ladder
Different questions demand different resolutions, matched to data availability:

- **Continuum shell model**: tissue-scale growth and pressure dynamics; analytically tractable; current workhorse for LumenoidGrowth manuscript.
- **Active polar fluid**: large-scale flow topology from constitutive assumptions; rapid phase-space exploration; current workhorse for Cell Shape Dynamics collective motion.
- **Vertex/Voronoi model**: individual cells, direct implementation of perturbation protocols, adhesion/contractility energies as explicit parameters; bridges molecular perturbations to tissue-scale observables; shared across all three projects.
- **Phase 2a SciML (parameter inference)**: infer constitutive parameters of known models from data; near-term, deployable once actin and cadherin field measurements are available at modest scale.
- **Phase 2b SciML (equation discovery)**: discover which terms and fields govern dynamics without assuming model form; longer-term, contingent on label-free data scale-up.

Coarser models generate predictions that motivate specific vertex-model implementations; vertex models produce synthetic datasets that validate SciML inference methods before applying them to real data.

### Scientific ML Pipeline
**Phase 2a — Parameter inference (near-term).** Given measured velocity **v**(x,t), actin intensity I_a(x,t), and cadherin intensities I_c(x,t): learn I_a → **p** and infer activity ζ, chirality κ, Frank constant K, traction γv₀ via sparse regression (assume model form, regress coefficients as functions of measured fields) or PINNs (embed PDEs as constraints, learn **p**(x,t) and parameters jointly). For the vertex model: learn E(cell state) — how junctional energy terms depend on molecular fields. Each perturbation condition produces a point in parameter space; the collection maps the cell energy/state landscape.

**Phase 2b — Equation discovery (longer-term).** Remove the assumption of a known model form. Sparse regression libraries or PINNs identify which terms in a general library of candidate equations (in actin, cadherin, polarity, curvature, cell-cycle phase) are actually necessary to reproduce the dynamics. This answers whether actin alone, or actin + cadherin, or some combination with mitosis is the minimal sufficient set of force descriptors. Contingent on the label-free segmentation and tracking pipeline reaching sufficient data volume.

Running Phase 2a → 2b across the perturbation series progressively triangulates the cell energy/state landscape — the low-dimensional structure determining which tissue behaviors are accessible and how they transition. Conceptually analogous to the morphodynamic atlas approach of Streichan & Vitelli and the active matter constitutive learning of Vitelli & Gardel.

### Shared Perturbation Logic
Growth rate is controlled through three independent routes: rapamycin (mTOR), glycolysis inhibitors (2-DG, metabolic), and Dhiraj's synthetic growth control circuits (direct cell-cycle). ZO-1 knockdown (including mosaic), actomyosin perturbations (ROCK1, MYH9, MYH10, pharmacological), and E-cadherin / N-cadherin induction and washout complete the shared battery. Each perturbation constrains all three models simultaneously. In the SciML framework, each condition is a sampling point in the cell state landscape; designing new perturbations is equivalent to choosing new sampling points to maximally discriminate between competing model structures.

### Geometry as a Control Variable
2D pre-patterning (stripes, concentric disks, squares) provides a qualitatively different perturbation — a change in boundary conditions and global symmetry rather than a molecular knockdown. Prior experiments establish that stripes seed hollow cylinders with two spherical caps and concentric rings seed toroidal lumenoids. This means 2D geometry controls 3D topological outcome, which is a constraint entirely orthogonal to molecular perturbations. In the active polar fluid model, different geometries impose different defect structures and flow patterns; in the Langevin Shape Dynamics framework, different geometries impose different effective boundary potentials on shape modes. Geometry perturbations are therefore simultaneously a test of the physical models and a tool for exploring regions of the shape/flow landscape inaccessible to spherical lumenoids.

## Synthetic Modules

A major arm of the program is the design and deployment of synthetic genetic circuits that give precise, tunable, and reversible control over cellular processes that are otherwise difficult to dissect pharmacologically. Four functional classes of modules are being developed or planned, each contributing to one or more projects through its perturbation and mosaic capabilities.

### Module Classes

**Fate control modules** drive cells toward specific identities — pluripotent, progenitor, or differentiated — or lock them in a particular transcriptional state. In the context of lumenoid morphogenesis, fate modules can set the starting composition of a mosaic lumenoid, bias which cells become apically vs. basally polarized, or test whether a specific fate transition is required for the 2D→3D morphogenetic step.

**Cell-cell communication modules** implement synthetic versions of intercellular signaling — lateral inhibition (synthetic Delta-Notch), juxtacrine feedback, or diffusible-signal sender/receiver pairs. These modules close the signaling-mechanics feedback loop that is currently modeled computationally in CellSorting but not yet implemented experimentally. Crucially, the fraction of cells carrying the sender vs. receiver construct is a tunable parameter, making mosaic compositions a direct experimental knob on circuit behavior.

**Adhesion and contractility modules** provide inducible, reversible, and dose-controlled access to the mechanical interaction parameters that all three projects model: E-cadherin, N-cadherin (already deployed in CellSorting), and the actomyosin regulators ROCK1, MYH9, MYH10. Unlike pharmacological perturbations, genetic circuits can be expressed in a defined cell fraction (mosaic), switched on and off with precise timing, and combined across multiple targets simultaneously in the same tissue.

**Mechano-biological circuits** are the most forward-looking class: circuits whose output (gene expression, protein activity) is regulated by the cell's mechanical environment — membrane tension, cytoskeletal strain, hydrostatic pressure, or substrate stiffness. These modules turn the tissue into a self-reporting system: they reveal when and where mechanical thresholds are exceeded, and they introduce mechano-biological feedback that can stabilize or destabilize morphogenetic trajectories.

### Connection to Projects

**LumenoidGrowth.** Dhiraj's synthetic growth control circuits (direct cell-cycle entry/exit) are already the third independent growth-rate knob, complementing rapamycin and glycolysis inhibitors. The key next additions are: (i) a mosaic growth circuit — expressing the growth-inhibiting construct in only a fraction of cells creates a spatially heterogeneous growth field, testing whether local growth suppression propagates into global shell mechanics or remains localized; (ii) a mechano-sensing growth feedback circuit — cells that sense compressive stress reduce their growth rate, testing whether the tissue regulates its own thickening through a mechanical feedback loop; (iii) a synthetic permeability circuit — inducible expression of a claudin or channel that alters tight-junction permeability, providing a cleaner and more reversible alternative to CRISPRi ZO-1 knockdown.

**Cell Shape Dynamics.** Synthetic modules contribute to this project primarily through polarity and mechano-sensing. A synthetic polarity circuit that biases a defined fraction of cells toward a specific actin orientation would directly test how the polarity field **p** is established and maintained — and how a locally injected polarity bias propagates through an active polar fluid. Mechano-sensing circuits responsive to shear stress or membrane curvature would reveal whether cells detect the flows they collectively generate, and whether this mechano-biological feedback acts as a stabilizing or amplifying mechanism for vortices and spirals. Timing is critical here: applying a polarity perturbation before vs. after the 2D→3D geometric transition tests whether the transition is gated by the polarity state. Mosaic expression of actomyosin perturbations (ROCK1, MYH9/10 circuits) creates spatial heterogeneity in the active stress field, directly testing how local mechanical defects reorganize global topological defect patterns — a much cleaner version of the pharmacological mosaic.

**CellSorting.** This is currently the project where synthetic modules are most developed. The DOX-inducible N-cadherin construct is the primary tool. The roadmap extends this in three directions. First, synthetic lateral inhibition (a sender that suppresses N-cadherin in contacting receivers) closes the signaling-mechanics feedback loop, enabling the salt-and-pepper pattern class predicted in theory but not yet accessible experimentally. Second, independent inducible control of adhesion (N-cadherin) and contractility (ROCK1 or MYH9) in the same cell — using orthogonal inducible promoters (DOX and, e.g., abscisic acid) — allows true independent titration of the adhesion and contractility axes of the sorting phase diagram, which cannot be achieved with either natural cadherins or pharmacological agents alone. Third, a mechano-sensing contractility circuit — one that upregulates actomyosin activity in response to increased heterotypic contact force — would create a mechanically self-reinforcing sorting boundary, testing whether domain interfaces are actively stabilized by feedback.

### Mosaic Expression as a Scientific Tool
Across all module classes, the ability to express a circuit in a defined, tunable fraction of cells (mosaic expression) is the most distinctive experimental capability synthetic modules provide. A mosaic lumenoid is not just a perturbed lumenoid — it is a tissue with built-in spatial heterogeneity that directly tests the local-to-global coupling in each physical model. In LumenoidGrowth, a mosaic growth circuit tests whether the continuum assumption of homogeneous shell mechanics holds. In Cell Shape Dynamics, a mosaic polarity or actomyosin circuit tests how locally perturbed cells reorganize global defect structure — the key question for the active polar fluid model with heterogeneous activity. In CellSorting, mosaic adhesion and contractility circuits sweep the sorting phase diagram in a single experiment by varying the expressing fraction rather than running separate induction levels.

### Timing Control
DOX-inducible circuits give access not just to the amplitude but to the *timing* of a perturbation. Switching on N-cadherin before vs. during vs. after colony formation, or before vs. after the 2D→3D transition, tests which morphogenetic transitions are sensitive to the mechanical/signaling state — a question that steady-state pharmacological perturbations cannot answer. In the SciML framework, timed perturbations generate non-equilibrium trajectories in the cell state landscape whose geometry encodes the direction and curvature of the energy landscape near specific morphogenetic checkpoints, providing richer constraints on E(cell state) than steady-state observations alone.
